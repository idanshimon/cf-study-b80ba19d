ContextForge Explainer

ContextForge is a small program that sits between your app and the LLM. It stores all your knowledge in a plain file (SQLite), and for each question it pulls ONLY the few relevant pieces into the LLM's context — instead of pasting everything. No special database, no AI training. Just pip install.

1. The Problem

An LLM (like ChatGPT or Copilot) has a context window — a limited amount of text it can read at once. If you have a big pile of knowledge (say, a hospital's 73 registry-field definitions, or 4,000, or a million), you CANNOT paste it all into every question. It's expensive and eventually doesn't fit.

The naive fix — pasting everything every time — gets linearly more expensive as your knowledge grows. ContextForge's claim: you can keep the cost flat no matter how big the knowledge gets. This explainer proves how.

2. Definitions

TOKEN A chunk of text the model counts and charges by. About ¾ of a word ("cardiogenic" ≈ 3 tokens).
Analogy: The ticks on a taxi meter.
CONTEXT WINDOW The model's short-term memory / desk space. It is limited, and you pay for everything sitting on the desk.
Analogy: A physical desk where you can only fit so many open books at once.
CORPUS Your whole pile of knowledge documents. In our example, a set of 73 field definitions.
Analogy: The entire library building.
BM25 A 40-year-old keyword-matching formula. It scores how well a document matches the words in your question. It is NOT AI — just counting word overlaps, weighted so rare words matter more.
Analogy: A librarian who checks the card catalog and hands you the 1-2 right books.
EMBEDDINGS The AI way to match text by MEANING rather than exactly matching words. ContextForge deliberately skips this — that's why it is fast and needs zero infrastructure, but also why it has one specific blind spot.

3. The Givens

Let's look at what we start with for our proof:

  • The Corpus: 73 registry field documents. Total size is 2,562 tokens. (If pasted whole, every question costs 2,562 tokens of "knowledge" just to set up).
  • The Question: "What SCAI stages are allowed for cardiogenic shock at admission?"

Here is one real document from the corpus — the "gold" document that actually answers our question. It is only 44 tokens long:

Registry: NY DOH-2254a Cardiogenic Shock Field: SCAI stage at admission (id: scai_stage_admit) Type: enum Allowed values: A | B | C | D | E Charted in: Cardiology assessment

4. The Walkthrough

How ContextForge answers this question, step by step.

1
Turn the question into keywords
[scai, stages, allowed, cardiogenic, shock, admission]

ContextForge throws away filler words ("what", "are", "for") and extracts exactly these 6 content words from the user's question.

2
Score every doc by keyword match (BM25)

10.97
scai_stage_max
10.82

5.11
admit_date / bnp_admit
3.65
map_admit
3.61

The document that literally contains "SCAI", "stage", and "admission" scores far above the rest. The librarian found the right book using weighted word counting. (Note the runner-ups share the word "admission", scoring above zero).

3
Load only the top slice

ContextForge puts only the top 20 documents (out of 73) into the prompt. The #1 document (our answer) is included. The resulting prompt size is 1,203 tokens (instead of 2,562 if we pasted all 73).

4
The model answers
"The allowed SCAI stages are A, B, C, D, or E."

The model reads the focused slice and returns the correct answer directly from the gold document.

5. The Lemma (The Small Result)

For this question, ContextForge sent 1,203 tokens instead of 2,562 tokens.

Savings ratio = 2,562 / 1,203 = 2.13x.

On 73 documents, this is a modest improvement. But it sets up the real power of the system.

6. The Theorem (The Big Result)

The savings grow without limit as the corpus grows. Because ContextForge always loads a fixed-size slice (the top ~20 relevant documents) regardless of how many documents exist, the prompt size stays CONSTANT while the knowledge base grows.

Documents Disk Size (Corpus) Prompt Sent to Model
100 4 KB 1,200 tokens
500 580 KB 1,200 tokens
1,500 1,692 KB 1,200 tokens
4,000 4,544 KB (1,136x increase) 1,200 tokens (1.00x flat)

The naive approach would have grown to ~1.1 MILLION tokens and stopped fitting in the model at all. This is the whole point: cost is decoupled from knowledge size. It's a library with a million books, but you only ever carry 2 to your desk.

7. Where the Proof Breaks

The theorem rests on Step 2 working correctly: BM25 must find the right document by matching WORDS. If the user asks the same thing using different words than the document uses, the word-overlap is zero and the librarian hands back nothing useful.

The Document
"...the longest acceptable interval between a patient's arrival and restoration of coronary blood flow is 88 minutes."
The Question
"What is the maximum allowed door-to-balloon time?"
Shared content words: ZERO

Because there are zero shared words, ContextForge does not retrieve the document. The answer is sitting in the corpus, unseen. This is the cost of skipping Embeddings (meaning-matching). ContextForge trades meaning-robustness for extreme speed and zero infrastructure.

Two more breakages in this family:

  • Aggregation: "Which patient had the HIGHEST lactate?" Keyword matching cannot rank or aggregate data across documents, and the model only ever sees a tiny slice, never the whole pile. Structurally unanswerable.
  • Absence: Asking about something not in the corpus (e.g. potassium). ContextForge will just return 20 unrelated documents that happen to score highest. It has no way to say "I don't have that."

8. The Takeaway

ContextForge is a librarian (BM25 keyword search) paired with a warehouse (a SQLite file). It keeps your LLM cheap and fast by carrying only the 1-2 relevant documents to the desk per question, and that savings grows without limit as your knowledge grows — PROVEN by the flat 1,200-token line against a 1,136x bigger corpus. The catch: the librarian matches WORDS, not MEANING, so it whiffs when you phrase a question differently than the document, or ask it to reason across the whole pile. Great for "look up the exact field/term"; not for "understand my whole dataset." Use it for fast lookup, and pair it with a smart model.
